Decentralization vs Commercialization: The Evolution and Impact of Externally Owned and Abstracted Accounts

16 September 2023

Every technological epoch introduces a game-changing shift that revolutionizes our digital interactions. The birth of email was such a transformative moment, anchoring our online identity and revolutionizing communication. The widespread adoption of wallets controlling Externally-owned Accounts (EOAs) signals a similar leap in the decentralized digital world, empowering users with the keys to their digital—and potentially physical—lives. While each step forward brings its own advantages and challenges, each trail blazes with short-term opportunity.

Email ushered in an era of personalized online experiences. EOAs follow a similar tale, providing new ways for users to interact within and beyond Blockchains. Their widespread adoption means users now comfortably manage their own keys. However, this scenario also uncovers certain motivations: major players keen to continue their growth, and struggling players keen to spark & retain growth. They are incentivized to use Abstracted Accounts to tether users to their ecosystems. Gnosis recently championed the cause of onboarding users onto Ethereum "by any means necessary," nudging our Overton window towards proprietary methods and walled gardens. To further compound this, numerous projects arise each year, built around storing keys in the browser in the name of "abstracting away signing", to get users through the door. This shift highlights an emerging conflict that goes beyond user experience. At its heart, it's a battle between the fundamental ethos of decentralization, which offers unique benefits to users versus:

  1. the aspirations of newly-established incumbents to monetize blockspace over all else,
  2. the desire of new entrants to get people into their application by any means necessary.

We currently, via EOAs, have the power to deliver personalized experiences for each user, in a similar way to individual email accounts, but without jeopardizing personal data or user security. Abstracted Accounts can provide significantly more abilities, and deliver an exceptional user-centric experience. However, with specialization comes a lack of adaptability. The varied rules linked to these accounts may lead to a fragmented Wallet space, with a myriad of user pockets each limited to their accounts' capabilities. And while the adaptability of Abstracted Accounts allows for refined user flows—akin to streamlined email providers—they can also be misused. The lurking danger is that such flexibility could accidentally or deliberately marginalize accounts types. Another way to phrase this is that they enable gatekeeping, and are a significant avenue for centralization.

Users may lose funds if contracts are not deployed across networks. Users may not be able to access projects if they aren't deployed on the same networks. Signing off-chain data becomes more convoluted than we have already made it. Projects and users may face de-facto censorship, and it further compounds confusion.

"My friend can do this, why can't I?"

Accounts may be permissioned in such a way to enforce compliance, and be limited to specific jurisdictions. Censorship. The proliferation of accounts which have these capabilities could hinder the immense potential of the decentralized world, and a precedent for this practice—attempting to placate regulators—has recently been set by Privacy Pools.

The evolution of email is analogous. The ways around the learning curves that came with its emergence ultimately led to the loss of freedom to choose. Self-hosting was deemed too hard, and ISPs effectively outlawed running home servers in a bid to control traffic. As a result, users have to work with a hosting provider or request (and pay for) a static IP. But to further compound this, major email service providers actively filter out emails originating from somebody who they do not deem trustworthy, such as those with self-hosted mailservers. While I imagine that this was done with the very very best of intentions, as most things are, we are now faced with a world where email protocols cannot be treated as open, and instead we have a cabal of providers.

Web3's mission must be to learn from these lessons, grapple with the risks of centralization, and understand that "improving UX" is not a brush that can sweep away all of our current frustrations. Improving UX is nuanced, involving making concerted efforts to understand and properly address user problems. EOAs will, regardless, remain in our future, and work must continue to both support the wallets that provide EOA functionality, and at a protocol level understand what we need to introduce to help our users, no matter how insurmountable the challenges may seem: key rotation capabilities, sponsored transactions, etc.

While I do believe that Abstracted Accounts offer significant near-term benefit, it's my conviction that this opportunity exists because the wallet & wallet-related-tooling space is severely lacking in quality. Users are consistently unable to connect to mobile wallets. Users consistently receive ghost notifications to sign messages. Users do not have full app capabilities to enable them to sign messages in-context. Users are forced to install a myriad of wallets. Users are forced to deal with sup-par experiences provided by sub-par software. Meanwhile, middlewares are already digging their moat around their previously-open infrastructure. If this is the current state of affairs, we cannot fall into the collective delusion that Abstracted Accounts will be the magic bullet to solve user problems. Much like Ethereum's L1 may become intrinsically tied to Lido if it does not take collective action, and SAFE having to understand whether it is intrinsically tied WorldCoin & OP due to the 2 million SAFE contracts that were deployed to Optimism, we cannot allow ourselves to become a victim of short-term incentives in lieu of hard, often subjective, work. The collective choices we make today will determine if the ecosystem stays true to its core decentralization principles or gradually shifts towards outright commercialization—and the centralization risk that comes with it.

Opinions will likely change as time progresses.
