Decentralization vs Commercialization: The Evolution and Impact of Externally Owned and Abstracted Accounts

16 September 2023

Every technological epoch introduces a game-changing shift that revolutionizes our digital interactions. The birth of email was such a transformative moment, anchoring our online identity and revolutionizing communication. The widespread adoption of wallets controlling Externally-owned Accounts (EOAs) signals a similar leap in the decentralized digital world, empowering users with the keys to their digital—and potentially physical—lives. While each step forward brings its own advantages and challenges, each trail blazes with short-term opportunity.

Email ushered in an era of personalized online experiences. EOAs follow a similar tale, providing new ways for users to interact within and beyond Blockchains. Their widespread adoption means users now comfortably manage their own keys. However, this scenario also uncovers certain motivations: major players keen to continue their growth, and struggling players keen to spark & retain growth. They are incentivized to use Abstracted Accounts to tether users to their ecosystems. Gnosis, a key player in the Ethereum field, recently championed the cause of onboarding users onto Ethereum "by any means necessary," nudging our Overton window towards proprietary methods and walled gardens. Numerous projects arise each year, generating an abstracted account of-sorts: storing keys in the browser, a major & well-documented security issue, provided as a UX feature, to get users through the door. This shift highlights an emerging conflict that goes beyond user experience. At its heart, it's a battle between the fundamental ethos of decentralization, which offers unique benefits to users versus:

  1. the aspirations of newly-established incumbents to monetize blockspace over all else,
  2. the desire of new entrants to get people into their application by any means necessary.

We currently, via EOAs, have the power to deliver personalized experiences for each user, similar to individual email accounts, but without jeopardizing personal data or user security. Abstracted Accounts can significantly expand upon this personalization, delivering an exceptional user-centric experience. However, with specialization comes a lack of adaptability. The varied rules linked to these accounts may lead to a fragmented Wallet space, with myriad user pockets each limited to their accounts' capabilities. And while the adaptability of Abstracted Accounts allows for refined user flows—akin to streamlined email providers—they can also be misused. The lurking danger is that such flexibility could accidentally or deliberately marginalize accounts, creating digital gatekeeping and a significant avenue for centralization.

Users may lose funds if contracts are not deployed across networks, and projects deployed to certain chains may face de-facto censorship as a result. Accounts may be permissioned in such a way to enforce compliances limited to specific jurisdictions—meaning censorship. The proliferation of accounts which have these capabilities could hinder the immense potential of the decentralized world. A precedent for this practice has recently been set by Privacy Pools, with Vitalik Buterin detailed as an Author.

Similarly, the evolution of email was a layered journey, with its learning curves and complexity, and ultimately, the loss of freedom to choose. Major email service providers may now filter out emails originating from a server which is not a part of the opaque & provider specific list of trusted providers. While I trust that this was done with the very very best of intentions, as most things are, we are now faced with a world where email protocols cannot be treated as open. Instead we have a cabal of providers. Web3's mission must be to learn from these lessons, grapple with the risks of centralization, and understand that "improving UX" is not a brush that can sweep away all of our current frustrations. Improving UX is nuanced, involving making concerted efforts to understand and properly address user problems. EOAs will, regardless, remain in our future, and work must continue to both support the wallets that provide EOA functionality, and at a protocol level understand what we need to introduce to help our users, no matter how insurmountable the challenges may seem: key rotation capabilities, sponsored transactions, etc.

While I personally do believe that Abstracted Accounts offer significant near-term benefit, it's my conviction that this opportunity exists because the wallet &wallet-related-tooling space is severely lacking in quality. Users are consistently unable to connect to mobile wallets. Users consistently receive ghost notifications to sign messages. Users do not have full app capabilities to enable them to sign messages in-context. Users are forced to install a myriad of wallets. Users are forced to deal with sup-par experiences provided by sub-par software. Meanwhile, middlewares are already digging their moat around their previously-open infrastructure. If this is the current state of affairs, we cannot fall into the collective delusion that Abstracted Accounts will be the magic bullet to solve user problems. Much like Ethereum L1 may become intrinsically tied to Lido if it does not take collective action and SAFE having to understand whether it is intrinsically tied WorldCoin due to the 2 million SAFE contracts that were deployed to Optimism, we cannot allow ourselves to become a victim of short-term incentives in lieu of hard, often subjective, work. The collective choices we make today will determine if the ecosystem stays true to its core decentralization principles or gradually shifts towards outright commercialization—and the centralization risk that comes with it.
